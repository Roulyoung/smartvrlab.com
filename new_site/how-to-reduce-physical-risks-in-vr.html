Virtual Reality Injuries: Physical Risks of VR and How to Reduce Them

adminsvr | March 29, 2021
Back to blog

Carpal tunnel syndrome, stiff shoulders, and eye-strain headaches are well-known consequences of prolonged computer use. But as more people move from traditional desktops into immersive environments, a new question arises: what are the physical risks of virtual reality (VR)?

Recent scientific research suggests that while VR unlocks powerful new possibilities for gaming, education, and professional training, it also introduces unique musculoskeletal risks that developers and users must take seriously.

Scientific study on VR-related muscle strain

A recent study conducted by Oregon State University examined how common virtual reality movements affect muscle strain and physical discomfort. The research aimed to establish early safety insights for a technology that is rapidly expanding beyond entertainment into education, healthcare, industry, and military training. The study was published in Applied Ergonomics with contributions from Northern Illinois University.

"There are no standards and guidelines for virtual and augmented reality interactions," said researcher Jay Kim from OSU's College of Public Health and Human Sciences. "We wanted to evaluate how target distance, location, and size affect the body so we can design safer interfaces."

How VR movements differ from traditional computer use

Unlike conventional computer work - where desks, armrests, and chairs provide physical support - virtual reality requires free-standing, full-body interaction. Users typically wear a headset and engage in extended arm movements, sustained shoulder elevation, continuous head and neck positioning, and repetitive pointing and gesturing. These factors significantly increase physical load compared to standard mouse-and-keyboard interaction.

Motion capture and muscle activity measurement in VR

To measure physical strain accurately, researchers used motion capture sensors on joints, electromyography (EMG) to measure muscle activity, and an Oculus Rift headset. Participants performed two common VR tasks: pointing at targets positioned around a circular interface and coloring virtual areas using finger movements. The tests were repeated with virtual objects placed at different vertical angles: eye level, 15 degrees above, 15 degrees below, and 30 degrees below eye level.

Key findings: shoulder, neck, and arm injuries in VR

Rapid onset of shoulder discomfort: extending the arm straight forward caused shoulder discomfort within three minutes. With prolonged VR sessions, this may lead to gorilla arm syndrome, rotator cuff injuries, or chronic shoulder fatigue.

Increased neck and cervical spine strain: the weight of VR headsets places additional stress on the cervical spine, increasing the risk of neck strain, postural fatigue, and long-term musculoskeletal issues.

Worst angles for VR interaction: targets 15 degrees above eye level produced the highest muscle activity and the most extreme neck and shoulder postures. Fifteen and thirty degrees below eye level reduced performance and increased head-tilt fatigue. Eye-level placement delivered the best balance between performance and comfort.

Ergonomic recommendations for safer VR design

  • Place frequently used objects close to the body.
  • Keep interactive elements at eye level.
  • Avoid sustained arm extension and elevated targets.
  • Minimize prolonged static postures.

These insights can help VR developers reduce injury risk and improve long-term usability.

Why this matters as VR adoption explodes

Tech analysts estimate that over 168 million people worldwide will use VR systems. While gaming represents a large portion of this market, VR is increasingly used in healthcare training, military simulations, education, and industrial safety training - for example, coal miners now use VR to safely practice dangerous procedures before entering real-world environments.

Learning from past technology mistakes

Jay Kim emphasizes the importance of acting early: "When personal computers emerged in the '80s and '90s, the health risks of overuse weren't fully understood until years later. With VR, we want to learn now rather than later." Early ergonomic standards could prevent widespread injury as VR becomes embedded in daily work and training.
