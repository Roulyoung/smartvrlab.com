What VR can do: 'Epidermal VR' gives technology a human touch

adminsvr | March 22, 2021
Back to blog

Imagine being able to feel a hold of a hand with a loved one miles away or a pat on the back from a teammate in an online game. That would be great, right?

Epidermal VR: touch you can wear

Researchers of Northwestern University have developed a new thin, wireless system that adds a sense of touch to any virtual reality (VR) experience. Beyond entertainment, the platform brings sensory feedback to prosthetics and gives telemedicine a human touch.

How the skin-like patch works

Referred to as an "epidermal VR" system, the device communicates touch through a fast, programmable array of miniature vibrating actuators embedded into a thin, soft, flexible material. The 15-centimeter-by-15-centimeter sheet-like prototypes comfortably laminate onto the curved surfaces of the skin without bulky batteries and cumbersome wires.

"People have contemplated this overall concept in the past, but without a clear basis for a realistic technology with the right set of characteristics or the proper form of scalability," said Northwestern's John A. Rogers. "We leveraged our knowledge in stretchable electronics and wireless power transfer to put together a superior collection of components designed as a skin-interfaced wearable device - with almost no encumbrances on the user."
"We are expanding the boundaries and capabilities of virtual and augmented reality," added Northwestern's Yonggang Huang. "By comparison to the eyes and the ears, the skin is a relatively underexplored sensory interface that could significantly enhance experiences."

Precision haptics with 32 actuators

Rogers and Huang's most sophisticated device incorporates a distributed array of 32 individually programmable, millimeter-scale actuators, each resonating around 200 cycles per second where the skin is most sensitive. Frequency and amplitude can be adjusted on the fly to maximize the vibratory force you feel.

Real-time touch over distance

The patch wirelessly connects to a touchscreen interface on a smartphone or tablet. When a user touches the screen, that pattern transmits instantly to the patch. Drawing an "X" on the screen, for example, produces a matching tactile pattern on the skin with negligible delay.

Where epidermal VR helps most

  • Long-distance connection: feel a handhold or pat on the back from afar.
  • Prosthetics: provide sensory feedback through comfortable wearable patches.
  • Telehealth: add human touch cues to remote care and rehabilitation.
  • Training and simulation: deliver realistic feedback in VR scenarios.

Wireless, battery-free comfort

The actuators sit in a slightly tacky silicone polymer that adheres without tape or straps. Wireless and battery-free, the device uses near-field communication (NFC) - the same technology in phone payments - to stay light and unobtrusive.

Giving technology a human touch

As epidermal VR matures, expect more comfortable, expressive haptics in VR and AR. Responsible handling of biometric touch data will be essential as the tech scales to full-body systems.
